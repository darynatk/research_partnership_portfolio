03 EXPERTISE
01 · Rigorous evaluation

I look at where models fail, not just how often.

At Hyfe, listening to the 20 hardest-classified cases by ear showed that a quarter of them were potentially mislabeled. The reported error rate wasn't measuring what it looked like it: I notice the difference between a real model limitation and noisy ground truth.

02 · Finding the real lever

I search where the gains are before adding complexity.

At Hyfe, I ran a controlled experiment to see whether a CNN's advantage came from its architecture or from the input representation it was fed. About 75% came from the input. That changed what I recommended for edge deployment.

03 · Segmentation with signal

I choose the cut that carries the most information.

At UN Women, an aggregate 61% figure said the energy crisis affected men and women "similarly." Breaking the data down by gender, age, and region showed 90% of the same respondents describing sharply gendered burdens. The segmentation was the finding.

04 CASE ONE · HYFE INC

A four-model comparison that turned secondary discoveries to primary product impact suggestions.

The task was to compare four model families for cough detection on the same 1.49M-clip dataset, under one frozen evaluation protocol, and establish which performs best. A convolutional neural network won cleanly. The ranking wasn't the interesting part though. What I got from looking at what the models got wrong turned out to matter more.

Finding 01

Was it the model, or how the data was fed to it?

The CNN's advantage could have come from its architecture, or from working on a richer input (the log-mel spectrogram) than the classical models saw. To find out, I retrained the same gradient boosting model on the CNN's exact spectrogram input. Only the model family differed. About 75% of the performance gap disappeared.

Where the CNN's advantage over the simpler model actually came from

~75% from how the data was fed in
~25% from the model itself
The Pipeline Layer Converting audio to log-mel spectrograms is computationally cheap and highly reusable.
The Architectural Layer The convolutional network added a massive footprint: roughly ×100 times slower per clip and highly restrictive for constrained on-device hardware.

Why this matters: A lighter model on the same input reached PR-AUC 0.80 vs the CNN's 0.90, at roughly 1/100 the compute per prediction. That is a real candidate for phones and low-power devices (Note: At strict operational thresholds, the representation accounts for roughly two-thirds of the gap).

Finding 02

Some of the "errors" weren't errors.

Forty-two coughs were missed by every model. Rather than report that as a failure rate, I ran a qualitative audit: I listened to twenty of them by ear and sorted each one. A quarter were clear coughs the test set had mislabelled. The models were penalised for being right. The takeaway: the reported error rate is a ceiling, not a measurement — some apparent failures are the model correctly overruling a bad label. I gave Hyfe a candidate list of test-set labels to reconsider.

42 coughs missed by all four models 20 audited by ear ~25% were mislabels, not misses
Finding 03

The advantage was really about
generalizing to unseen data.

The tabular models had learned the acoustic fingerprints of the specific microphones in the training set. When tested on recording sources they hadn't seen, their performance dropped by 0.40+ PR-AUC. The CNN dropped only 0.08. It was learning the sound itself, not the recording setup.

Validation-to-test drop — tabular models −0.40 to −0.44 CNN −0.08

The through-line: The last two findings weren't in the brief. They came from treating the errors as data, instead of just counting on the result.

CNN · PR-AUC 0.90 · 94% sensitivity at 95% specificity Held-out test set · 29,510 clips Lightweight GBM on spectrograms · PR-AUC 0.80 · ~100× faster Leakage-safe grouped CV · bootstrap 95% CIs

One finding from this study shows that granular analysis can actually change policies.. While 61% of respondents initially reported that the crisis impacted men and women equally, digging beneath the surface revealed a different reality: 90% of those same households experienced severe disparities in care and domestic workloads. My analysis accounted for these systemic differences by breaking down the data to ensure resources matched actual human needs:

  • • I looked past the headline number to see where the burden actually landed: older women, rural mothers, and women with disabilities in the southeast.
  • • Different subgroups faced very different pressures — the "average Ukrainian woman" wasn't a useful unit for policy.
  • • The specific segments shaped the report's final recommendations for who to prioritize with resources..

06 RESEARCH PUBLICATIONS
Quantitative

What Ukrainian Teenagers Know About Online Safety. What do They Ignore?

A survey was the right tool for this study because the question was about prevalence: how widespread is risky behaviour, and does knowing the risks change it? I helped develop the questionnaire to separate knowledge from behaviour, so the gap between them could be measured directly.

The main finding was that most teens name the threats of Russian FSB's cyberattacks, yet act unsafely anyway.

n = 253 teens (14–18), representative nationwide sample· Original analysis and sole authorship · Texty.org.ua, Oct 2025

Mixed methods

The Drivers of AWOL: Why Personnel Leave and What Brings Them Back

This population couldn't be sampled cleanly since going AWOL is a criminal offense. I combined two instruments to fit that constraint: an anonymous online survey to get honest answers about a prosecutable act, and a few in-depth interviews with those who agreed to open up about a painful subject.

The study challenged a common assumption about AWOL: 79% of respondents had voluntarily joined the military, suggesting that the key issue is not who serves, but the conditions under which some decide to leave.

n = 128 anonymous + 4 in-depth interviews · Sole interviewer & author; Original analysis · Texty.org.ua, Jan 2025

Qualitative

How War Changes the Families Veterans Come Home To

Veterans and their families are a sensitive population, and I didn't have baseline data to design a survey on how reintegration is experienced at home. So I used in-depth interviews, and I chose to focus not on the individual veteran but on the whole family unit as a social structure where the reintegration actually plays out.

That framing made visible that the caregiving and emotional labor that partners and parents were doing was mostly uncounted.

08 HOW I WORK

I start by distrusting the question. Many projects fail quietly at the beginning: the wrong thing gets prioritized, the sample skips the people who matter, the labels encode someone's assumption. No model can recover from that. So before I model anything, I want to know what the data is actually measuring, who it left out, and where it's misleading.

Only then do I build the pipelines and train the models. I treat the measurement as the part most likely to be wrong, because in my experience it usually is. Get that right, and the modeling is often the easy part. Get it wrong, and the modeling just launders the error.
